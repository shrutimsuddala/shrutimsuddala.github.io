Your Data Rights Explained:
What Companies Must Tell You

Privacy policies are designed to be unread. But inside them — and in the laws that govern them — are rights most consumers never exercise. Here's what you are actually entitled to know and demand.

By Shruti Suddala 9 min read Consumer Rights

The average privacy policy takes 18 minutes to read. Most people spend zero. This information asymmetry — companies knowing exactly what they collect and consumers knowing almost nothing — is precisely what modern data protection legislation attempts to correct. The rights it creates are substantial. The challenge is knowing they exist.

This article focuses on the rights that matter most in practice: the right to know what is collected, the right to access it, the right to request deletion, and the right to object to certain uses. Whether you are covered by GDPR in Europe, privacy legislation in India, or equivalent frameworks in other regions, the core principles are increasingly consistent.

Key Takeaways
  • You have the right to request a full copy of all personal data a company holds about you.
  • Companies must respond to access requests within defined timeframes — often 30 days.
  • The right to deletion ("right to be forgotten") has real limitations — not all data can be erased.
  • You can opt out of your data being used for direct marketing or sold to third parties.
  • Filing a complaint with a data protection authority is free and often effective.

The Right to Know

Before a company collects your personal data, they are generally required to tell you what data they are collecting, why they are collecting it, how long they will keep it, and who they will share it with. This obligation — known as transparency — is the foundation of modern data protection law.

In practice, this information appears in privacy policies and cookie notices. These documents are legally required but rarely written to be understood. If you want to know specifically what data a company holds about you, the right to access is the more powerful tool.

Subject Access Requests: Your Right to See Your Data

A Subject Access Request (SAR) is a formal request to a company asking them to provide you with all personal data they hold about you. Under most modern data protection frameworks, companies are legally required to respond — typically within 30 days — and cannot charge a fee for a first request.

What a SAR Should Include

  • A copy of all personal data held about you
  • The purposes for which the data is being processed
  • The categories of data being held
  • Details of any third parties the data has been shared with
  • The retention period or criteria used to determine how long data is kept

"Data is the raw material of the information economy. Your personal data is your property. The law is slowly catching up to that principle."

The Right to Deletion

Often called the "right to be forgotten," the right to erasure allows you to request that a company delete your personal data. This right is not absolute — companies can refuse if they need the data to fulfil a contract, comply with a legal obligation, or for other legitimate reasons.

However, in many situations — particularly where consent was the basis for processing and you withdraw that consent, or where your data was collected for marketing purposes — deletion requests must be honoured.

When Deletion Can Be Refused

  • The data is needed to complete a transaction or fulfil a contract with you
  • Retention is required by law (tax records, employment records, etc.)
  • The data is necessary for the establishment or defence of legal claims
  • Processing is in the public interest (research, journalism, public health)

The Right to Object

You have the right to object to your personal data being used for direct marketing at any time — and companies must stop using it for that purpose, with no exceptions. This includes targeted advertising based on your browsing history or purchasing behaviour.

For other types of processing based on "legitimate interests," you can also object, but the company can override your objection if they can demonstrate compelling legitimate grounds that override your interests.

How to Exercise Your Rights

Most major companies now provide self-service tools for data requests in their privacy settings or account dashboards. If not, you can submit a request in writing to their data protection officer — an email is sufficient.

If a company fails to respond within the required timeframe, provides an inadequate response, or refuses a request without sufficient legal justification, you can escalate to your national data protection authority. Complaints are free to file, and authorities have the power to investigate and impose significant fines on non-compliant organisations.
